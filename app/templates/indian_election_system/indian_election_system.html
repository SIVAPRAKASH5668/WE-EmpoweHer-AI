Indian Electoral System

The Indian Electoral System is the constitutional framework that governs elections in India, the world's largest democracy. The system manages elections for the Lok Sabha (lower house), Rajya Sabha (upper house), state legislatures, and various other local bodies.

History

The electoral system in India was established with the adoption of the Constitution in 1950. The first general elections were held between 1951 and 1952, marking the beginning of democratic governance in independent India. The Election Commission of India was established on January 25, 1950, a day before India became a republic.[1]

Key Historical Developments

  • 1951-52: First general elections conducted using ballot papers
  • 1989: Introduction of voter ID cards
  • 1998: Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) introduced
  • 2014: Introduction of VVPAT (Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail)

Electoral Structure

The Indian electoral structure is organized at multiple levels:

Parliamentary Elections

The Parliament of India consists of two houses:

  • Lok Sabha: 543 elected members from single-member constituencies
  • Rajya Sabha: 245 members, with 233 elected by state legislatures

State Elections

Each state has its own legislative assembly (Vidhan Sabha) with members elected from single-member constituencies. Some states also have a legislative council (Vidhan Parishad).

Election Commission

The Election Commission of India (ECI) is an autonomous constitutional authority responsible for administering election processes. It consists of:

  • Chief Election Commissioner
  • Two Election Commissioners

Powers and Functions

  • Supervising elections
  • Maintaining electoral rolls
  • Setting election schedules
  • Implementing Model Code of Conduct
  • Recognition of political parties

Electoral Process

The electoral process involves several stages:

Pre-Election Phase

  • Delimitation of constituencies
  • Voter registration
  • Nomination of candidates
  • Scrutiny of nominations
  • Election campaign

Election Phase

  • Polling day arrangements
  • Electronic voting
  • Security measures
  • Vote counting
  • Declaration of results

Voting System

India follows the First-Past-the-Post (FPTP) voting system, where:

  • Each voter casts one vote
  • Candidate with highest number of votes wins
  • No minimum percentage required

Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs)

EVMs have been used nationwide since 2004, featuring:

  • Tamper-proof design
  • Battery operation
  • Maximum 2000 votes per unit
  • VVPAT integration for verification

Electoral Reforms

Major reforms implemented include:

  • Introduction of EVMs and VVPAT
  • Criminalization of seeking votes on religious grounds
  • Mandatory disclosure of criminal records
  • Limits on campaign expenditure
  • Right to reject candidates (NOTA)

Challenges and Criticisms

The system faces several challenges:

  • Money power in elections
  • Criminalization of politics
  • Voter registration issues
  • EVM tampering concerns
  • Electoral roll discrepancies
